The Thought Process Behind: Manifold Optimization on the Magnitude Torus for Fourier Phase Retrieval

Created on July 07, 2026

2026   ·   math   code   ·   publications

The work “Manifold Optimization on the Magnitude Torus for Fourier Phase Retrieval” is accepted to ICIP 2026. This blog post is a record of how we discovered this topic, what our initial motivation is, and what approaches we have taken that led to the finalized conference version.

Motivation

Working under Prof. Homer H. Chen on optical systems, it is quite a far stretch for us to suddenly stumble upon the problem of Fourier phase retrieval. This part goes over exactly the backstory.

Furthermore, we also give a brief description of our motivation to analyze the Fourier phase retrieval problem from a geometric point of view.

Optical interpolation

Originally, we were considering the following problem: how can we use a simple optical system to enhance the resolution of a display? No matter how high th resolution a digital image is, the perceived resolution or clarity of the image is inherently limited by the resolution of the physical display. The importance of such problem need not to be further emphasized.

An approach that we came up with was to utilize a “4\(f\)-system” to physically realize the interpolation operation.


See the figure above. The input field \(U\) at \(0f\) comes from the left of the system, and after passing through a multiplicative modulation \(X\) at \(2f\), the output field \(Z\) exits from the right at \(4f\). By the principles of Fourier optics, a \(2f\) lens system (from the front focal plane of the convex lens to its rear focal plane) can be seen as performing a Fourier transform.

The image above shows the resulting output field \(Z\) if the input field \(U\) is coherent. For incoherent inputs, the output field becomes

\[\left|Z(\boldsymbol{x})\right|^2 = \left|U(\boldsymbol{x})\right|^2 * \left|\mathscr{F}\{X\}(\boldsymbol{x})\right|^2,\]

where \(*\) is the convolution operation. Therefore, the task of designing a suitable \(X\) that creates high-resolution output images boils down to the classical Fourier phase retrieval problem.

To reiterate, the (discrete) Fourier phase retrieval problem is that given amplitude measurements \(\boldsymbol{y} \in \mathbb{R}_{\ge0}^{n\times1}\), the \(d\)-dimensional \(n\times n\) discrete Fourier transform matrix \(A\), and a signal domain constraint set \(\mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n\times1}\), find a signal \(\mathbb{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times1}\) that satisfies

\[\begin{align*} &\left|A \boldsymbol{x}\right| = \boldsymbol{y}, \\ &\text{s.t. } \boldsymbol{x} \in \mathcal{B}. \end{align*}\]

For our study, we primarily worked with the nonnegative signal constraint \(\mathcal{B} = \mathbb{R}_{\ge0}^{n\times1}\). The details can be seen from our work (Perng et al., 2026).

Projected gradient descent

Many algorithms were proposed to tackle the phase retrieval problem, and they have extremely excellent performances! However, what bugs me is that I do not fully understand why these algorithms work so well beyond local heuristic explanations, and in fact, neither do most people.1

My instinct when solving this problem is not the Gerchberg—Saxton (GS) algorithm or the hybrid-input-output algorithm, but instead the gradient descent algorithm. I firmly believe that geometry is the way to understand algorithms, and what better way to understand gradient descent via plotting its optimization landscape.

First, we need to introduce a loss function for we optimize over. Here we used the magnitude-based loss function

\[\ell(\boldsymbol{x}) := \left\|\, |A\boldsymbol{x}| - \boldsymbol{y}\right\|_2^2.\]

By performing projected gradient descent over the loss function \(\ell\), the iterations will converge to local minima. It should be noted that the GS algorithm is equivalent to the projected gradient descent with stepsize \(\mu = 0.5\).

Sometimes, the algorithm is successful, and we can losslessly retrieve the signal (up to its trivial associates), but sometimes, the iterates stalls at weird places.

Take a look at the example above. Although the iteration eventually converges, there is this huge plateau that the iterates got stuck on. From this, it immediately occurred to me that the loss landscape of \(\ell\) and its intersection with \(\mathcal{B}\) must be really complicated.

At low dimensions, e.g., \(n = 3\), I was able to plot the loss landscape for the true one-dimensional signal \(\boldsymbol{x} = [0,1,2]\).


The black points marked are the trivial associates to \(\boldsymbol{x}\). And would you look at that! There exist nontrivial associates and they form a ring (a one-dimensional torus). I thought to myself, “I must know more about its geometry.”

The Magnitude Torus

The magnitude torus associated with the magnitude measurement \(\boldsymbol{y}\) is defined as

\[\mathbb{A}_{\boldsymbol{y}} = \left\{ \mathbb{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times1} \middle\vert \,\left|A \boldsymbol{x}\right| = \boldsymbol{y}\right\}.\]

It didn’t occur to us that this set is in fact a torus. In hindsight, this is quite trivial as we can easily assign

\[\boldsymbol{x} = A^{-1}(\boldsymbol{y} \odot \exp(\mathrm{i}\boldsymbol{\phi})),\]

while ensuring that the resulting \(\boldsymbol{x}\) is real. The remaining task is to actually characterize what symmetries the phases \(\boldsymbol{\phi}\) should satisfy before we can understand the dimensionality of the magnitude torus. Our exploration, however, took a completely different route.

Local dynamical point of view

From the one-dimensional signal with \(n=3\) we have seen earlier, it is believed that the solutions to \(|A\boldsymbol{x}| = \boldsymbol{y}\) is connected, maybe there is a way to traverse on such a set! This idea is later shown to be incorrect as we see from the paper that the magnitude torus consists of multiple disjoint tori. Nevertheless, let us see where this idea leads us to.

If multiple zeros to \(\ell\) are connected, then the shape of the landscape should locally be like a valley. I.e.,

\[\dim\left( \mathrm{ker}\left( \nabla^2\ell(\boldsymbol{x})\right)\right) > 0\;\;\;\;\;(\ell(\boldsymbol{x}) = 0),\]

where \(\nabla^2\ell(\boldsymbol{x})\) is the Hessian. Note that the directions in the kernel of the Hessian are the directions that leads from a zero of \(\ell\) to another.

The Hessian of \(\ell\) at an arbitrary point is derived to be

\[\begin{aligned} \nabla^2\ell(\boldsymbol{x}) &= \mathbf{Re}\left\{A^{\mathsf{H}} \mathrm{diag}\left(2\cdot\boldsymbol{1} - \boldsymbol{y} \odot |A \boldsymbol{x}|^{-1}\right) A \right\} \\ &\hspace{0.5cm}+ \mathbf{Re}\left\{A^{\mathsf{H}} \mathrm{diag}\left(\mathrm{sgn}(A \boldsymbol{x})^{2} \odot \boldsymbol{y} \odot |A \boldsymbol{x}|^{-1}\right) A^{*}\right\}. \end{aligned}\]

Note that \(\boldsymbol{1}\) is the vector of ones. Its evaluation at a zero \(\boldsymbol{x}^\star\) can be readily calculated to be

\[\boxed{ \nabla^2\ell(\boldsymbol{x}^\star) = I + \underbrace{\mathrm{Re}\left\{A^{-1} \mathrm{diag}\left(\mathrm{sgn}(A \boldsymbol{x}^\star)^{2}\right) A^{*}\right\}}_{M(\boldsymbol{x}^\star)} }.\]

Positive-semidefiniteness of the Hessian

From a simple MATLAB calculation, we see that the spectrum of \(\nabla^2\ell\) at zeros are really simple: almost half of the eigenvalues are 0’s, and the other half are 2’s. To be more precise, for two-dimensional signals of size \(n = M \times N\), we have the following classification

\((M,N)\) # of 0’s # of 2’s
(even,even) \(\frac{MN-4}{2}\) \(\frac{MN+4}{2}\)
(even,odd) \(\frac{MN-2}{2}\) \(\frac{MN+2}{2}\)
(odd,odd) \(\frac{MN-1}{2}\) \(\frac{MN+1}{2}\)

Can we show that these observations are true for all possibilities? Immediately from the boxed equation above, we can see that the loss function is positive-semidefinite at the global minima: for any real vector \(\boldsymbol{v}\), the second term can be bounded by

\[\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{h}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{Re}\left\{A^{\mathsf{H}} \mathrm{diag}\left(\mathrm{sgn}(A \boldsymbol{x}^\star)^{2}\right) A^{*}\right\} \boldsymbol{h} &= \boldsymbol{h}^\mathsf{T} \mathbf{Re}\left\{F^{\mathsf{T}} \mathrm{diag}\left(\mathrm{sgn}(A^* \boldsymbol{x}^\star)^{2}\right) A\right\} \boldsymbol{h} \\ &= \mathbf{Re}\left\{(A \boldsymbol{h})^{\mathsf{T}} \mathrm{diag}\left(\mathrm{sgn}(A^* \boldsymbol{x}^\star)^{2}\right) (A \boldsymbol{h})\right\} \\ &\ge -\boldsymbol{h}^\mathsf{T} \boldsymbol{h} \end{align*}\]

Henceforth, the Hessian $\nabla^2\ell(\boldsymbol{x}^\star)$ is positive-semidefinite. The equality holds if and only if $\mathrm{sgn}(A\boldsymbol{h})^2 \odot \mathrm{sgn}(A^*\boldsymbol{x}^\star)^2 = -\boldsymbol{1}$.

Tangent vectors

Next, to find out the spectrum exactly, we must find the eigenvectors. By a miraculous observation, we tested out calculating \(\nabla^2 \ell(\boldsymbol{x}^\star) [\boldsymbol{x}^\star] = 2 \boldsymbol{x}^\star\)! Interestingly enough, the global minima \(\boldsymbol{x}^\star\) is itself an eigenvector of eigenvalue 2! This inspires us to calculate \(\nabla^2 \ell(\boldsymbol{x}^\star) [P\boldsymbol{x}^\star]\), where \(P\) is a translation of the image \(\boldsymbol{x}^\star\).

Since \(P\) is a translation, its action on \(\boldsymbol{x}^\star\) is equivalent to a phase shift in the Fourier domain. Therefore, we can introduce a phase \(\boldsymbol{\phi}_P\) that satisfies

\[A(P\boldsymbol{x}^\star) = (A\boldsymbol{x}^\star) \odot \mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i} \boldsymbol{\phi}_P}.\]

Continue on with the calculations:

\[\begin{aligned} M(\boldsymbol{x}^\star) \cdot P\boldsymbol{x}^\star &= \mathbf{Re}\left\{A^{-1} \mathrm{diag}\left(\mathrm{sgn}(A \boldsymbol{x})^{2}\right) A^{*}P\boldsymbol{x}\right\} \\ &= \mathbf{Re}\left\{A^{-1} \mathrm{diag}\left(\mathrm{sgn}(A \boldsymbol{x})^{2}\right) \left((A^*\boldsymbol{x}) \odot \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\boldsymbol{\phi}_P}\right)\right\} \\ &= \mathbf{Re}\left\{A^{-1} \left((A\boldsymbol{x}) \odot \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\boldsymbol{\phi}_P}\right)\right\} \\ &= \mathbf{Re}\left\{A^{-1} AP^{-1} \boldsymbol{x}\right\} \\ &= P^{-1} \boldsymbol{x}. \end{aligned}\]

The second to last equality is by the fact that $\boldsymbol{\phi}_{P^{-1}} = -\boldsymbol{\phi}_P$. We can thus construct the eigenvectors to be

\[\begin{align*} \nabla^2\ell(\boldsymbol{x}^\star) \left[P \boldsymbol{x}^\star + P^{-1} \boldsymbol{x}^\star\right] &= 2 \left(P \boldsymbol{x}^\star + P^{-1} \boldsymbol{x}^\star\right), \\ \nabla^2\ell(\boldsymbol{x}^\star) \left[P \boldsymbol{x}^\star - P^{-1} \boldsymbol{x}^\star\right] &= \boldsymbol{0} && (P\boldsymbol{x}^\star \neq P^{-1}\boldsymbol{x}^\star). \end{align*}\]

The vectors \((P-P^{-1}) \boldsymbol{x}^\star\) are the tangent vectors to the magnitude torus as described by Barnett et al.2 Given them, we are able to traverse all path-connected zeros of \(\ell\) to \(\boldsymbol{x}^\star\).

To be precise, the curve

\[\exp\left((P - P^{-1})t\right) \boldsymbol{x}^\star\]

lies on the magnitude torus for all translation \(P\) and all \(t\).

A new basis

The matrix

\[\exp\left((P - P^{-1})t\right)\]

is, however, non-periodic. In fact, we can find out that it has multiple components all oscillating in a different frequency by performing spectral decomposition. What are the eigenvectors of \(P\)? They are, coincidentally, the Fourier vectors \(\boldsymbol{a}_{k}\)! That is how we finally obtained the basis vectors

\[\{\mathbf{Re}\{\boldsymbol{a}_k\}, \mathbf{Im}\{\boldsymbol{v}_k\}\}_{k \in \mathcal{N}_{p} \cup \mathcal{N}_{c}} = \{\boldsymbol{u}_k, \boldsymbol{v}_k\}_{k \in \mathcal{N}_{p} \cup \mathcal{N}_{c}}\]

seen in our work.

Dimensions of the magnitude torus

The dimension of the magnitude torus is equal to number of unique eigenvectors \((P - P^{-1}) \boldsymbol{x}^\star\).

Global

From the local dynamical point of view, we see that the magnitude torus is indeed a surface traced by mutually orthogonal circles, i.e., it is a torus.

The global point of view, on the other hand, treats the magnitude torus as the intersection of mutually orthogonal hyperplanes and hypercylinders. This view gives us an explicit count of the number of tori and their dimensions.

Traversing the torus

Having obtained the basis \(\{\boldsymbol{u}_k, \boldsymbol{v}_k\}_{k \in \mathcal{N}_{p} \cup \mathcal{N}_{c}}\), we can move on the magnitude torus via

\[\begin{aligned} &\exp\left((\boldsymbol{u}_k \boldsymbol{v}_k^\mathsf{T} - \boldsymbol{v}_k \boldsymbol{u}_k^\mathsf{T})t\right) \boldsymbol{x}^\star, &k \in \mathcal{N}_{c}. \end{aligned}\]

The actual computation can be made simpler by using the Rodrigue’s formula (or Euler’s identity for \(\mathfrak{so}(3)\)) or by separating the action on each coordinates in the torus parameterization. The latter is way faster in implementations.

A fun thing demonstrating the torus parameterization is to interpolate between two points on the magnitude torus. Below we show one such example where we linearly interpolate between the horizontally shifted trivial associates. The figure on the right shows the torus angles \(t_k\) for \(k\in\mathcal{N}_{c}\).

Manifold Optimization

In hindsight, the two algorithms we have developed are quite obvious—it is natural that such algorithms can lead the iterations closer to the local optima. Yet, coming up with them took us on quite a convoluted thought process.

TorusGD

The idea of TorusGD is simple, perform gradient descent in the torus coordinates, this idea is an immediate consequence of the parameterization we obtained that allows us to traverse on the torus.

The problem lies in how we can numerically implement it.

The first task is to obtain the basis \(\{\boldsymbol{u}_k, \boldsymbol{v}_k\}_{k \in \mathcal{N}_{p} \cup \mathcal{N}_{c}}\), i.e., how do we obtain the row indices \(\mathcal{N}_{p}\) and \(\mathcal{N}_{c}\) that define unique Fourier constraints on the set of possible signals?

The MATLAB code we used was created by ChatGPT. Its logic is simple, a vector \(\boldsymbol{a}_k\) is created by taking the Kronecker product of its one-dimensional Fourier vector components, each of length equal to the corresponding size \(N_k\) (\(k \in [D]\)): Using the notation in our paper, we have \(n = N_1 \cdot \ldots \cdot N_D\), and let

\[\boldsymbol{f}^{(\ell)}_{k} = [1,\omega_k,\omega_k^2,\ldots,\omega_k^{N_\ell-1}]^\mathsf{T}, \omega_{k} = \exp\left(\mathrm{i}\frac{2\pi}{N_\ell}k\right),\]

then the corresponding Fourier vector is

\[\boldsymbol{a}_k = \bigotimes_{\ell=1}^{d} \boldsymbol{f}^{(\ell)}_{k_\ell}\]

Next, let us discuss how the indices \(\mathcal{N}_p\) and \(\mathcal{N}_{c}\) are determined. Suppose \(D=2\), \(N_1=M\), and \(N_2 = M\). We denote the Fourier vector by \(\boldsymbol{a}_{(k,\ell)} = \boldsymbol{f}^{(1)}_{k} \otimes \boldsymbol{f}^{(2)}_{\ell}\). In the code, phi stands for \(\boldsymbol{a}\), u{k+1} stands for \(\boldsymbol{f}^{(1)}_{k}\), and v{l+1} stands for \(\boldsymbol{f}^{(2)}_{\ell}\).

for k = 0:M-1
    for l = 0:N-1

        k2 = mod(-k, M);
        l2 = mod(-l, N);

        phi = u{k+1}.' * v{l+1};

        % -------- plane (self-conjugate) -------- 
        if k==k2 && l==l2
            idx_p = idx_p + 1;
            vp{idx_p} = reshape(real(phi), [M * N, 1]);

        % -------- cylinder (conjugate pairs) --------
        elseif (k < k2) || (k==k2 && l < l2)
            idx_c = idx_c + 1;

            vc{idx_c,1} = reshape(real(phi), [M * N, 1]);
            vc{idx_c,2} = reshape(imag(phi), [M * N, 1]);
        end
    end
end

See the code excerpt above. An index \((k,\ell)\) is in \(\mathcal{N}_{p}\) if both \(\boldsymbol{f}^{(1)}_k\) and \(\boldsymbol{f}^{(2)}_\ell\) are real. This is determined by the following condition.

if k==k2 && l==l2

Check that the above condition holds for \(M\) and \(N\) being either even or odd.

On the other hand, an index \((k,\ell) \in \mathcal{N}_{c}\) comes in conjugate pair. Therefore, we only pick one in the pair. This is implemented by the condition

elseif (k < k2) || (k==k2 && l < l2)

This condition states that we keep \((k,\ell)\) if \(\boldsymbol{f}^{(1)}_k\) is not real and \(k < M/2\), or if \(\boldsymbol{f}^{(1)}_k\) is real but \(\boldsymbol{f}^{(2)}_\ell\) is not and \(\ell < N/2\). This concludes the explanation to our code.

dualPGD

The need for coming up with another algorithm other than being satisfied with TorusGD is that TorusGD runs really slow. Not only slow, but the precalculations and storage of the \(\{\boldsymbol{u}_k,\boldsymbol{v}_k\}\) are resource demanding. In fact, we cannot even run image signals larger than \(41\times41\) on our laptops.

The algorithm we finally came up with predates the name dualPGD. It is only after the algorithm was formulated did we realize that it was in fact dual to the PGD algorithm.

Its motivation is from our exploration of the gradient flow limit for TorusGD, where \(\mu \rightarrow 0\): Let \(\widetilde{\boldsymbol{x}}\) be the gradient flow and \(s\) be the time parameter, i.e., \(\widetilde{\boldsymbol{x}} = \boldsymbol{x}(\boldsymbol{t}(s))\). The gradient flow over \(\mathbb{T}^d\) is

\[\frac{\mathrm{d} \boldsymbol{t}}{\mathrm{d} s} := - \nabla(F\circ \boldsymbol{x})(\boldsymbol{t}(s)).\]

The induced flow over the magnitude torus is

\[\frac{\mathrm{d} \widetilde{\boldsymbol{x}}}{\mathrm{d} s} = \boldsymbol{x}_{*,\boldsymbol{t}}\left(\frac{\mathrm{d} \boldsymbol{t}}{\mathrm{d} s}\right),\]

where \(\boldsymbol{x}_{*,\boldsymbol{t}}\) is the pushforward of torus parameterization, i.e.,

\[\boldsymbol{x}_{*,\boldsymbol{t}}:T_{\mathbb{t}}\mathbb{T}^d \rightarrow T_{\boldsymbol{x}(\mathbb{t})}\mathbb{A}_{\boldsymbol{y}}.\]

It is at this moment that it suddenly occurred to me that what if I simply replace the pushforward map by the projection onto the tangent space of \(\mathbb{A}_{\boldsymbol{y}}\)? By having \(\boldsymbol{x}_{k+1} = P_{\mathcal{A}}(\boldsymbol{x}_{k} + \boldsymbol{v})\), we can simply let \(\boldsymbol{v}\) be a suitable descent direction to obtain another phase retrieval algorithm.

Having this idea in mind, it was only a few calculations away to make sure that \(P_{\mathcal{A}}\) is a retraction and that the resulting algorithm is the dual to the PGD.

NOTE

When we were proving that \(P_{\mathcal{A}}\) is a retraction, it is required from the steps that we have

\[N_{\boldsymbol{x}} = \mathrm{ker}\left(\mathbf{Im}\left\{\mathrm{diag}(A\boldsymbol{x}) A^*\right\}\right).\]

This is how the explicit characterization of the normal space was discovered.

Comparing the two algorithms

Below is an illustration of the two algorithms.


The two algorithms operate under different metric. If we let the stepsize \(\mu \rightarrow 0\), then the two gradient descent algorithms becomes gradient flow. We can compare them in this regime.

For TorusGD, we have its gradient flow being

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{\mathrm{d} \widetilde{\boldsymbol{x}}}{\mathrm{d} s} &= \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d} s} \left(\sum_{k\in\mathcal{N}_{p}} s_k \boldsymbol{u}_k + \sum_{k\in\mathcal{N}_{c}} 2 y_k \left(\cos t_k \boldsymbol{u}_k + \sin t_k \boldsymbol{v}_k\right)\right) \\ &= \sum_{k\in\mathcal{N}_{c}} 2 y_k \left(-\sin t_k \boldsymbol{u}_k + \cos t_k \boldsymbol{v}_k\right) \frac{\mathrm{d} t_k}{\mathrm{d} s} \\ &= \underbrace{\mathrm{row}\left\{\frac{\partial \boldsymbol{x}}{\partial t_k}\right\}_{k \in \mathcal{N}_{c}}}_{S} \frac{\mathrm{d} \boldsymbol{t}}{\mathrm{d} s} \\ &= S\left(-\nabla (F\circ \boldsymbol{x})\right) \\ &= - S \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial \boldsymbol{x}} \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{x}}{\partial \boldsymbol{t}}\right)^\mathsf{T} \\ &= - SS^\mathsf{T} \nabla F, \end{aligned}\]

where \(\nabla F := (\partial F/\partial\boldsymbol{x})^\mathsf{T}\). On the other hand, for dualPGD,

\[\frac{\mathrm{d} \widetilde{\boldsymbol{x}}}{\mathrm{d} s} = - \mathrm{proj}_{T_{\widetilde{\boldsymbol{x}}} \mathbb{A}_{\boldsymbol{y}}}\left(\nabla F(\boldsymbol{x})\right).\]

To understand their relationship, let us expand \(SS^\mathsf{T}\):

\[\begin{aligned} SS^\mathsf{T} &= \mathrm{row}\left\{\frac{\partial \boldsymbol{x}}{\partial t_k}\right\}_{k \in \mathcal{N}_{c}} \mathrm{col}\left\{\left(\frac{\partial \boldsymbol{x}}{\partial t_k}\right)^\mathsf{T}\right\}_{k \in \mathcal{N}_{c}} \\ &= \sum_{k \in \mathcal{N}_{c}} 2y_k^2 \boldsymbol{\tau}_k \boldsymbol{\tau}_k^\mathsf{T} \end{aligned}\]

where

\[\boldsymbol{\tau}_k = \sqrt{2} \left(-\sin t_k \boldsymbol{u}_k + \cos t_k \boldsymbol{v}_k\right)\]

is the \(k\)th unit tangent vector at \(\boldsymbol{x}\). On the other hand,

\[\begin{aligned} \mathrm{proj}_{T_{\widetilde{\boldsymbol{x}}}} &= \sum_{k \in \mathcal{N}_c} \boldsymbol{\tau}_k \boldsymbol{\tau}_k^\mathsf{T}. \end{aligned}\]

From these calculations above, it is easy to see that the two flows are different. Yet, their difference is simply a scaling by \(2y_k^2\) along the \(\boldsymbol{\tau}_k\) direction for \(k \in \mathcal{N}_{c}\).

Stationarity Condition

When we talk about the stationarity condition, we meant the conditions that \(\boldsymbol{x}\) should hold if and only if either TorusGD or dualPGD halts under small stepsizes.

The requirement that stepsizes should be small reflects that our domain, the magnitude torus, is not convex. Though small stepsizes may have you stay at a stationary point, large stepsizes may take you to its antipodal point on the torus.


Look at the figure above, which shows the change of position after an iteration of dualPGD with varying stepsizes \(\mu\). Note that the point \(\boldsymbol{x}\) here is NOT a global minima, but instead a local minima.

On the other hand, the figure below shows the case where \(\boldsymbol{x}\) here IS near a global minima. The two behaviors are obviously different. How to utilize this observation, however, is a task for another day.


For both the pictures above, we are considering the positive signal constraint. The result is quite universal for different test signals that satisfies the uniqueness condition3. We suspect this behavior has something to do with the contact geometry between \(\mathbb{A}_{\boldsymbol{y}}\) and \(\mathcal{B}\) at the point, and the jump is due to the jumping to antipodal points.

Some more interesting thing to consider: The stationarity condition for dualPGD is

\[\begin{aligned} &\mathbf{Im}\left\{(A\boldsymbol{x})^* \odot (A \nabla F)\right\} = \boldsymbol{0} \\ &\mathbf{Im}\left\{(A\boldsymbol{x})^* \odot (A (\alpha \boldsymbol{x} + \beta\nabla F))\right\} = \boldsymbol{0} \\ &\Leftrightarrow \forall k\in[n] \begin{dcases} \mathrm{sgn}(\boldsymbol{a}_k^\mathsf{H} \boldsymbol{x}) = \mathrm{sgn}(\boldsymbol{a}_k^\mathsf{H} (\alpha \boldsymbol{x} + \beta\nabla F)), \\ \mathrm{sgn}(\boldsymbol{a}_k^\mathsf{H} \boldsymbol{x}) = \mathrm{sgn}(\boldsymbol{a}_k^\mathsf{H} (\alpha \boldsymbol{x} + \beta\nabla F)) + \pi, \\ \boldsymbol{a}_k^\mathsf{H} (\alpha \boldsymbol{x} + \beta\nabla F) = 0 \end{dcases} \end{aligned}\]

for all \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\). If it is case 1 for all \(k \in [n]\), then the latter statement is equivalent to

\[\begin{aligned} &\Leftrightarrow \mathrm{sgn}(A \boldsymbol{x}) = \mathrm{sgn}(A (\alpha \boldsymbol{x} + \beta\nabla F)) \\ &\Leftrightarrow \boldsymbol{y} \odot \mathrm{sgn}(A \boldsymbol{x}) = \boldsymbol{y} \odot \mathrm{sgn}(A (\alpha \boldsymbol{x} + \beta\nabla F)) \\ &\Leftrightarrow A^{-1} \left(\boldsymbol{y} \odot \mathrm{sgn}(A \boldsymbol{x})\right) = A^{-1} \left(\boldsymbol{y} \odot \mathrm{sgn}(A (\alpha \boldsymbol{x} + \beta\nabla F))\right) \\ &\Leftrightarrow \underbrace{P_{\mathcal{A}}(\boldsymbol{x})}_{\stackrel{!}{=}\boldsymbol{x}} = P_{\mathcal{A}}(\alpha \boldsymbol{x} + \beta\nabla F). \end{aligned}\]

For \(\alpha = 1\) and \(\beta = -\mu\), we recover the stationarity condition of TorusGD.

NOTE

What about the other two cases? And what about other values of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\)?

We do not currently have a satisfactory answer for the two questions above. However, as far as simulated experiments and our geometric intuitions have shown, we can at least conclude that the stationarity condition for TorusGD and dualPGD coincide.
